# Running Social App — Product & Technical Guide

## 1. Project Overview

We are building a **mobile-first social running app for Spain** focused on helping people **find compatible runners, join communities, and participate in real-world running meetups and events**.

This is **not** a training-first product and **not** a Strava clone.

The core idea is simple:

> help people run together, more often, with less friction.

The product should make it easier to:

- discover runners with similar level and goals,
- join local communities and crews,
- organize and attend running meetups,
- prepare races with other people,
- give hosts, captains, and creators better tools to grow real communities.

---

## 2. Product Vision

Become the reference mobile app for **social running communities in Spain**.

When a user thinks:

- “I want people to run with”
- “I want a group near me”
- “I want to join a running crew”
- “I want to organize runs more easily”
- “I want to see who is going to this race”

this app should be the first place they think of.

---

## 3. Core Thesis

The market already has:

- tracking apps,
- race calendars,
- WhatsApp groups,
- Instagram communities,
- Strava clubs,
- event platforms,
- coaching apps.

What is still fragmented is the layer between:

- **people**
- **community**
- **local discovery**
- **real-world organization**

Our bet is that there is room for a product built around:

> **social coordination for running**, not just activity logging.

---

## 4. Problem Statement

Runners today face several friction points:

### 4.1 Finding compatible people is difficult
It is hard to find runners who match in:

- pace,
- schedule,
- location,
- motivation,
- social vibe.

### 4.2 Communities are fragmented across tools
Groups are often split between:

- WhatsApp,
- Instagram,
- Strava,
- spreadsheets,
- DMs,
- random event pages.

### 4.3 Organizing meetups is messy
Even small weekly runs require too much manual coordination.

### 4.4 Race participation is not socially connected enough
A lot of runners join races, but the social experience before and after the race is poorly structured.

### 4.5 Creators and captains lack proper tools
Influencers, hosts, and community leaders often have reach, but not enough product support to convert that into ongoing community participation.

---

## 5. Product Positioning

We are not building:

- a pure running tracker,
- an advanced coaching tool,
- a generic social network,
- a race registration platform,
- an enterprise event management product.

We are building:

> a mobile platform for **social running discovery, community participation, and event coordination**.

### Positioning statement

A mobile app for runners who want to **find their people**, join local crews, and turn running into a more social and repeatable part of their life.

---

## 6. Product Principles

## 6.1 Mobile-first
The product is designed for mobile only.
There will be no user-facing web product in the initial phase.

## 6.2 Social before tracking
The main value comes from community and coordination, not from deep sports analytics.

## 6.3 Real-world outcomes matter most
The product exists to trigger offline action:

- joining,
- meeting,
- running,
- returning,
- belonging.

## 6.4 Simplicity wins
We prefer a focused and emotionally clear product over a feature-heavy app.

## 6.5 Community leaders matter
Captains, crew leaders, and creators are key leverage points.
If they win, the network grows faster.

## 6.6 Local density is more important than broad reach
A strong city-level or neighborhood-level network is more valuable than shallow national presence.

---

## 7. Target Users

## 7.1 Primary user
Amateur/popular runners who:

- run somewhat regularly,
- want company,
- are interested in local running communities,
- care about vibe and compatibility,
- may already use Strava but still lack a social coordination layer.

## 7.2 Secondary users

### Hosts / Captains
People who organize weekly runs, communities, or local crews.

### Creators / Influencers
People with an audience in running, fitness, or lifestyle who could activate communities.

### Race-driven runners
Users who want to prepare races with others or see who else is attending.

---

## 8. What Makes This Product Different

The differentiation is not “better tracking”.

The differentiation is:

### 8.1 Compatibility-based discovery
Find runners by:

- pace,
- level,
- city/zone,
- schedule,
- goals,
- vibe/community type.

### 8.2 Community-native product design
Communities are not an add-on.
They are a central product object.

### 8.3 Events as a first-class primitive
Runs, meetups, shakeouts, recovery runs, race-day plans, and group sessions should be easy to create and join.

### 8.4 Race-social layer
Races should not only exist as external events.
They should become social anchors around which people gather and connect.

### 8.5 Tools for leaders
We build not only for participants, but also for the people who create momentum.

---

## 9. Reference Products

We are not copying one product.
We are combining strong patterns from multiple references.

## 9.1 Strava
Use as inspiration for:

- running credibility,
- activity/feed patterns,
- club legitimacy,
- sport-oriented identity.

Do not copy:

- tracking-first product philosophy,
- overly metric-heavy experience as core UX.

## 9.2 Nike Run Club
Use as inspiration for:

- clean and motivating onboarding,
- emotional tone,
- simple and premium-feeling UI,
- aspirational but approachable product feel.

Do not copy:

- highly individual experience as the product center.

## 9.3 Meetup
Use as inspiration for:

- group discovery,
- local participation,
- event attendance structure,
- lightweight community coordination.

Do not copy:

- horizontal/general-purpose feeling,
- visually outdated or overly utilitarian patterns.

## 9.4 Geneva
Use as inspiration for:

- sense of belonging,
- community identity,
- emotionally alive group spaces.

Do not copy:

- chat-app-first structure.

## 9.5 Partiful
Use as inspiration for:

- event pages,
- RSVP flow,
- host-friendly event presentation,
- social shareability,
- events that feel exciting.

Do not copy:

- event-only value without deeper community structure.

## 10. Experience Goals

The app should feel:

- alive,
- social,
- sporty,
- urban,
- modern,
- welcoming,
- credible inside running culture.

The user should quickly feel:

- “there are people like me here”
- “I can join something today”

- “this is about running with others, not just tracking myself”

## 11. MVP Goal

The MVP is built to validate one core question:

> Will runners use a mobile app to discover compatible people, join communities, and attend running meetups?

If the answer is yes, we can deepen the social layer, community tools, and race features.

---

## 12. MVP Scope

## 12.1 Onboarding
Collect enough signal to personalize the experience:

- name
- username
- avatar
- city / area
- approximate running level
- typical pace range
- usual distance
- preferred schedule
- goals
- social intent

## 12.2 Runner discovery
Show runners compatible by:

- city / area
- pace
- level
- availability
- goals

## 12.3 Communities
Allow users to:

- create communities,
- join communities,
- leave communities,
- view community details,
- see community members,
- identify captain/admin roles.

## 12.4 Events / Meetups
Allow users to:

- create a run/event,
- join an event,
- view attendees,
- see pace/level expectations,
- see meetup point and timing.

## 12.5 Profiles
Each user should have:

- personal info,
- running identity,
- goals,
- communities,
- upcoming events.

## 12.6 Race layer
Basic support for races:

- create or add a race,
- mark attendance/interest,
- see who from your communities is going,
- use races as social coordination anchors.

---

## 13. Explicitly Out of Scope for MVP

We will not prioritize:

- advanced in-app running tracking,
- segments,
- deep performance analytics,
- advanced messaging/chat systems,
- web product for users,
- advanced admin dashboards,
- payments/subscriptions,
- creator monetization,
- wearable integrations beyond basic future-ready planning,
- complex gamification systems.

---

## 14. Product Strategy for Integrations

## 14.1 Strava strategy
Strava is useful as an optional context layer, not as the core of the product.

### Why integrate later
A Strava connection can help with:

- onboarding,
- importing activity context,
- reducing friction,
- validating running profile data.

### Why not depend on it
The product must remain strong even without Strava.
Our unique value is in:

- communities,
- events,
- social coordination,
- local discovery.

### Product rule
We build our own social layer first.
External fitness integrations are secondary.

---

## 15. Technical Strategy

## 15.1 Product architecture
This is a **mobile-only user product** in the first phase.

We will build:

- a mobile client,
- a backend API,
- a relational database,
- optional shared packages.

We will not build:

- a user-facing website,
- a web dashboard in MVP.

## 16.3 Infrastructure
- **VPS**
- **Docker / Docker Compose**
- **API under a subdomain**
- self-hosted Postgres and backend containers

---

## 17. Why This Stack

## 17.1 Expo
Best fit for a fast mobile-first build with good developer experience and low setup friction.

## 17.2 Hono
Lightweight, modern, TypeScript-friendly, easy to reason about, and enough for a clean API layer.

## 17.3 tRPC
Fits the preference for end-to-end type safety and fast iteration.

## 17.4 Better Auth
Strong self-hosted option without locking into a paid SaaS early.
Fits well with the current stack choices.

## 17.5 PostgreSQL
The product domain is relational by nature.
Users, communities, memberships, events, and attendance are all naturally relational.

## 17.6 Drizzle
Keeps the data layer explicit, lightweight, and TypeScript-friendly.

## 20. UX Priorities

## 20.1 Home should drive action

The home screen should answer:

* who can I run with?
* what is happening near me?
* which communities fit me?
* what event should I join next?

## 20.2 Profiles should be socially useful

Profiles are not just identity pages.
They should help answer:

* what level is this person?
* what do they want?
* are they compatible with me?
* what communities are they part of?

## 20.3 Event creation should be lightweight

Creating a meetup must feel easy and natural.

## 20.4 Communities should feel alive

A community should feel like a real running group, not just a static page.

## 20.5 Visual density must stay low

The product should avoid the feel of a cluttered dashboard.

---

## 21. UI Direction

## 21.1 Visual language

The app should feel:

* sporty
* social
* urban
* modern
* premium but not elitist
* warm but not childish

## 21.2 Practical design guidelines

* strong typography hierarchy
* clean cards
* large touch targets
* prominent join / RSVP actions
* restrained use of data
* photos, avatars, maps, and real-world context matter
* avoid enterprise/admin-like layouts
* avoid generic “fitness dashboard” patterns

## 21.3 Main inspiration mix

Recommended inspiration balance:

* **Strava** for running legitimacy
* **Partiful** for event UX
* **Geneva** for community atmosphere
* **Nike Run Club** for tone and onboarding

## 22. Success Metrics

In MVP, we care about behavior more than vanity metrics.

### Core metrics

* onboarding completion rate
* percentage of users who join at least one community
* percentage of users who RSVP to at least one event
* weekly active users
* repeat attendance to events
* communities created
* events created
* user retention after first social action

### Strong signals of product-market pull

* people return to check new meetups
* hosts create repeated events
* communities grow organically
* users invite others
* races become social anchors in-app

---

## 23. MVP Success Criteria

The MVP is successful if:

* users understand the product quickly,
* users join communities,
* users attend or RSVP to meetups,
* there is repeated social usage,
* some communities show signs of becoming self-sustaining,
* organizers find the product genuinely useful.
